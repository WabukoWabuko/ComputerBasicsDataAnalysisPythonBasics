Core Concept

1. The "Big Three" (Mean, Median, Mode)

A Guru knows that the Average (Mean) can be a lie if there are "Outliers."

Example: 5 people earn $20k, but the Boss earns $500k.
Average: $100k (Misleading!)
Median: $20k (The Truth)

Teaching Tip: Ask the student to calculate the median of a list of numbers where one number is 100x larger than the rest.

2. Standard Deviation & Variance

This measures Consistency. In the market, high variance means high risk.

Low Variance (Stable)

High Variance (Volatile)

The Analyst's Bible

3. Correlation vs. Causation

Just because two things happen together doesn't mean one caused the other.

The Classic Guru Example: Ice cream sales and Shark attacks both go up in the summer. Does ice cream cause shark attacks? No. The hidden factor (lurking variable) is Heat.

4. Probability Distributions

Common distributions: Normal, Binomial, Poisson. Choosing the right model matters for inference and prediction.

Normal: $$f(x)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$$

Example: Heights are approximately Normal; counting daily defects fits a Poisson model.

5. Hypothesis Testing & Confidence Intervals

Formulate a null hypothesis, choose an alpha (commonly 0.05), compute a test statistic and p-value, then decide whether to reject the null.

Example: Test whether a new feature increases conversion rate. Use a two-proportion z-test and report a 95% CI for the lift.

Teaching tip: Emphasize effect size and sample power ‚Äî small p-values are not automatically practically significant.

6. Practical Exercises

  1. Given a CSV of monthly sales, compute moving averages, seasonal decomposition, and forecast with a simple linear model.
  2. Simulate 10,000 samples from a Normal distribution and show the sampling distribution of the mean.
  3. Perform an A/B test analysis: compute conversion lift, CI, and required sample size for 80% power.
